Dear [contact name],

I am writing to ask the Society's permission for a small, non-commercial local-history project.

I have built a free website that overlays the 1839 Abbots Langley tithe map on a present-day map, so
the historic field pattern can be compared directly with the modern village. A slider fades between
the two, and every numbered plot can be searched and clicked to show its tithe-apportionment record
(landowner, occupier, field name, land use, and acreage). It is intended purely as a public resource
for people interested in the parish's history, with no advertising and no charge.

The project draws on two pieces of the Society's work:

1. the Society's digital images of the tithe map (the scans behind the Zoomify viewer at
   eforms.org.uk/allhstithemap), which I have georeferenced so they line up with modern mapping; and
2. the Society's transcription of the tithe apportionment (the
   `AbbotsLangleyTitheAward_corrected` spreadsheet), which supplies each plot's record.

I understand both carry the Society's copyright, and I would not publish either without your
agreement. **I am asking permission to host these online, with clear attribution to Abbots Langley
Local History Society.** My intended credit, shown on the site and adjustable to your preference, is:

> Tithe map and apportionment data © Abbots Langley Local History Society, reproduced by permission.
> Original records held at The National Archives (IR 29 and IR 30).

A few points that may help your decision:

- The site is **not yet public.** I have set up a private, password-protected preview so the Society
  can see exactly what would be published before deciding. You are very welcome to review it: I can
  send a link and add the email addresses of anyone who should have access. [preview link, if ready]
- It is **non-commercial and free to host**, and I am happy to take it down or amend it at any time
  at the Society's request.
- I would gladly **link back to the Society's website** (allhs.org.uk) and credit named contributors
  however you wish.
- If the Society holds **higher-resolution scans** of the map sheets, they would noticeably improve
  the overlay; but I can work with what is already published if not.

Could you let me know:

- whether the Society is willing to grant permission for this use;
- the exact attribution wording you would like; and
- any conditions or restrictions I should observe.

Thank you for the Society's work in digitising and transcribing these records. It is a wonderful
resource, and I hope this project can help share it more widely. I am happy to discuss any of the
above, or to demonstrate the preview, at your convenience.

With thanks and best wishes,

Anthony Underwood
[your email] [optional contact details]

## Notes for you (not part of the letter)

- **Copyright nuance worth knowing:** the *original* 1839 tithe map and apportionment are public
  records held at The National Archives (references IR 30/15/1 and IR 29/15/1) and are out of
  copyright. What the Society holds rights in is its *digitisation* of the map and its *transcription*
  of the apportionment, i.e. the specific files you are reusing. Seeking permission is therefore both
  the courteous course and the practical one, since it is the Society's scans and spreadsheet you are
  building on. The letter reflects this without labouring the point.
- **Before sending:** fill `[date]`, `[contact name]`, `[your email]`, and the `[preview link]`
  (your gated subdomain). Confirm whoever you address it to is the right person (the Society's
  secretary or web contact is usually best).
- **Leverage:** offering the live preview is your strongest card. Seeing their own material as a
  polished, working map tends to turn a "maybe" into a "yes", and may prompt them to share better
  scans.
